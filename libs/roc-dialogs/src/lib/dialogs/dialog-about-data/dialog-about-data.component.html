Return On College(ROC) leverages data from a variety of public and private sources. Using the most up-to-date information from education and workforce agencies allows ROC to develop the most accurate, individualized, and comprehensive return-on-investement estimates for all possible education and career pathways. With support from the U.S. Department of Education, ROC was built over three years by a team of application developers, data scientists, and education researchers with deep expertise in modeling education and career options.

The data on careers, colleges, job opportunities, living expenses, tuition and fees, and more that power ROC come from these sources:

College Scorecard

The U.S. Department of Education's College Scorecard was built for users to search and compare colleges, majors, costs, admissions, and debt levels for colleges and universities in the United States. In addition to providing you with basic information on colleges, we use Scorecard data to model average debt and earnings for the field of study (major) and colleges you chose.

Visit College Scorecard: https://collegescorecard.ed.gov/

Common Core of Data

The U.S. Department of Education's Common Core of Data includes the Public and Private School Directories. This allows you to select your high school and links you to college and career opportunities in your area.

Visit the Common Core of Data: https://nces.ed.gov/ccd/

Emsi : Labor Market Analytics

Since 2001, Emsi has grown to be the world's leading provider of labor-market data. Emsi combines existing governement data with hundreds of millions of employer job postings to see what careers are available, what education they require, what they pay, and where they are located. We use this information to model your career outcomes in cities across the nation.

Visit Emsi: https://www.economicmodeling.com/

Integrated Postsecondary Education Data System (IPEDS)

IPEDS is the U.S. Department of Education's central source for information on colleges and universities. We use IPEDS to show admissions criteria; what degree programs and majors are offered at what campuses; college's tuition and fees, grants and scholarships, and net price for specific income levels and living situations; college's retention and graduation rates; and more. These data help us build the most accurate estimate of your cost of attendance and expected time to a degree.

Visit IPEDS: https://nces.ed.gov/ipeds/

O*Net, U.S. Department of Labor

O*net is the U.S. Department of Labor's primary occupational data system. We use O*Net to gather information on over 1,000 occupations and link these jobs to majors and programs of study to help guide your education-to-career pathway.

Visit O*Net: https://www.onetonline.org/

U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics (BLS)

BLS provides a host of useful information on labor markets and local living expenses. We use BLS data on ..., as well as the Consumer Expenditure Surveys to show you average costs for rent, food, and other expenses for cities across the nation.

Visit Bureau of Labor Statistics: https://www.bls.gov/

U.S. Census Bureau

The U.S. Census Bureau hosts the American Community Survey (ACS), which annually captures important demographic and economic information on your community. We use ACS to understand what typical education levels, incomes, and expenditures are in cities across the nation - and how these values change over time.

Visit the American Community Survey: https://www.census.gov/programs-surveys/acs

In addition to these sources, we also consult with various offices and divisions at the U.S. Department of Education to ensure our calculations reflect the most up-to-date information on programs and eligibility criteria.
